Finding Professional Help

The first step in managing dementia is securing an accurate diagnosis from a qualified medical professional. This often involves a team of specialists.

  • Primary Care Physician (PCP): Your starting point for initial cognitive screening and referrals.
  • Neurologist: A specialist in brain disorders who can diagnose specific types of dementia.
  • Geriatrician: A doctor specializing in the health and diseases of older adults.
  • Neuropsychologist: Conducts detailed tests on memory and thinking skills to assess cognitive function.

Community & Support

Connecting with others provides emotional support and practical advice. Support groups are invaluable for sharing experiences and reducing feelings of isolation.

  • For Individuals with Dementia: Groups focused on living well with cognitive change, sharing coping strategies, and engaging in social activities.
  • For Caregivers: A safe space to discuss challenges, learn from others, and find emotional support.
  • Online Forums: 24/7 access to a global community for advice and encouragement.

Frequently Asked Questions

Where should I start if I suspect a loved one has dementia?

The best first step is to schedule an appointment with their primary care physician. The doctor can conduct an initial assessment and refer you to a specialist, like a neurologist, for more comprehensive testing.

How do I find a reputable support group?

Local hospitals, national organizations (like the Alzheimer's Association), and your doctor's office are excellent sources for recommendations on trusted local and online support groups.

Are there financial assistance programs available for care?

Yes, various government and non-profit programs can help with the cost of care. Research options like Medicare, Medicaid, and programs through the Department of Veterans Affairs if applicable. Social workers can be a great resource for navigating these systems.
