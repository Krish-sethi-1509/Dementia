What is Dementia?

Dementia describes a group of conditions characterized by significant impairment of at least two brain functions, typically including memory loss, impaired language, judgment, and reasoning skills. The changes are usually progressive and irreversible, impacting independence and quality of life.

Alzheimer’s disease is the most common cause, but dementia can arise from vascular problems, mixed causes, or rarer brain disorders.

Symptoms & Early Signs

  • Losing short-term memory, repeating questions or stories
  • Misplacing objects, getting lost in familiar places
  • Difficulty planning, problem-solving, or making decisions
  • Struggling to speak, write, or follow conversations
  • Changes in mood, behavior, or personality
  • Confusion about time, events, or surroundings

Main Types & Causes

  • Alzheimer’s Disease: Marked by amyloid plaques & tau tangles disrupting brain communication.
  • Vascular Dementia: Blocked or reduced blood flow from stroke or vessel disease.
  • Lewy Body Dementia: Deposits of abnormal proteins causing fluctuating awareness, hallucinations, and movement symptoms.
  • Frontotemporal Dementia (FTD): Damage to frontal and temporal lobes affecting personality and language.

Diagnosis & Living Well

Seek Assessment: Medical exam, memory/cognitive tests, possibly brain scans.
Early Intervention: Address treatable causes and manage symptoms.
Support Plans: Cognitive training, physical/social activity, and care planning.
Community: Access support groups, counseling, and care services to improve quality of life.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is dementia a normal part of aging?

No. While minor memory lapses can happen with age, dementia is caused by brain disease, not normal aging.

Can dementia be reversed?

Most types are irreversible, but some conditions with similar symptoms (like vitamin deficiencies or thyroid disorders) can be treated.

What's the difference between Dementia & Alzheimer’s?

Dementia is an umbrella term for symptoms. Alzheimer’s is a specific disease, and the most common cause of dementia.
